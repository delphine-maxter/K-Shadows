Temple Street Night Market

Named for the nearby Tin Hau Temple, Temple Street has evolved into Hong Kong’s most storied night market—a neon-lit corridor known for street food and fortune tellers, active nightly with the best atmosphere after sunset.

Fortune tellers say some clients leave no shadow beneath the lamps—an omen that the reading is for more than one life.

Tin Hau Temple (Yau Ma Tei)

The temple complex—among Kowloon’s largest—dates to the mid-19th century, dedicated to Tin Hau, Goddess of the Sea and includes halls once used for worship, arbitration and free schooling for boat and land people.

Spirits of drowned sailors linger near the altar; fortune tellers report strange presences after dark.

Yau Ma Tei Theatre

Built in 1930 and revived in 2012 as a Cantonese opera venue, this is Kowloon’s only surviving pre–World War II theatre. Its Art Deco façade and timber roof trusses frame a rare link between cinema and opera heritage.

Projectionists reported phantom applause and flickering lights in empty halls.

Shanghai Street (Tong Lau Row)

The pre-war shophouses (tong lau) at Nos. 600–626 are Grade I historic buildings. Tong lau typify Hong Kong’s urban past: shopfronts at street level, living quarters above, verandahs over arcades—evolving from 19th-century forms to Art Deco-influenced blocks by the 1930s.

Wartime screams said to echo in abandoned upper floors; lullabies from families who slept above their shops.

Mei Ho House

The last surviving H-block of the Shek Kip Mei resettlement estate, Mei Ho House was completed in 1954 after the devastating Christmas Day 1953 fire displaced over 53,000 residents. Today it operates as a youth hostel and museum interpreting early public housing life.

Residents once spoke of spirits roaming cramped corridors at night.

Man Fat Seafood Restaurant

Opened in the 1970s as a modest dai pai dong-style eatery in Sham Shui Po. Became famous for live seafood tanks, signature dishes like typhoon shelter crab, and affordable banquets. Featured in local media as a nostalgic spot preserving old-school Hong Kong dining culture.

During the Hungry Ghost Festival, locals joke that “hungry spirits” join midnight feasts, especially when tables are full and laughter echoes late into the night.
